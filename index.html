CORE PRINCIPLES OF SURVEY EXECUTION

• Successfully interviewing is an art and should not be treated as a mechanical process.


• The art of interviewing develops with practice, but there are certain basic principles, which, if followed, will help you become successful.



ASKING QUESTIONS/ RECORDING RESPONSES & CORRECTING MISTAKES.

Ask each question exactly as it is written in the instrument.(slowly and clearly

So that the respondent will have no difficulty hearing and understanding.)

*However, sections with additional instructions should be read aloud to the respondent.

*In some cases, you may have to ask additional questions to obtain a complete answer from a respondent(probing);

*Record responses in a legible manner that can be read by you and others

(Never leave a response blank! a blank is recorded as “missing information”

because it is not known whether you asked the question or not. If a response is

negative, the negative response must be circled.)


• Checking completed survey forms- This review should be done before the respondent leaves, to ensure that every appropriate question was asked, that all answers are clear and reasonable, and that the handwriting is legible. Minor corrections can be made, but any serious errors should be clarified by the respondent.

• The enumerator should simply explain to the respondent that he/she made an error and ask the question again.

• Enumerators should record ALL information on the survey forms that have been provided to them. Any calculations they make should be written in the margins or on the back of the questionnaires.


TIPS FOR INTERVIEW

  • • Be neutral throughout the interview.
  • • Never suggest answers to the respondent.
  • • Do not change the wording or sequence of questions.
  • • Handle hesitant respondents tactfully.
  • • Do not form expectations.
  • • Do not hurry the interview
  • • Finally, it is important to demonstrate punctuality, enthusiasm, and dedication.

BUILDING RAPPORT

Activities to be executed on survey include;

• Stress confidentiality of responses. If the respondent is hesitant about responding to the interview or asks what the data will be used for, explain that the information you collect will remain confidential, no individual names will be used for any purpose, and all information will be grouped together to write a report. You should never mention other interviewers or show completed questionnaires to other enumerators or Team leaders in front of a respondent or any other person.


• Answer all questions from the respondent frankly: before agreeing to be interviewed, the respondent may ask you some questions about the survey or why he/she was selected to be interviewed. Be direct and pleasant when you answer.

• Minimize distractions as much as possible. The presence of other people or on-going activities during an interview can prevent you from getting frank, honest answers from a respondent. Many respondents change what they say, or simply say less, when other people are present.

• Reassure staff that individuals are not being used for administrative purposes. During the introduction, reassure them that the data is not being used for promotions or sanctions, and no individuals will be identified in the study(stress confidentiality).

• Minimize interference with the day’s work. Make sure that the work that needs to be done at the data collection center is duly interrupted because of the interviews or observations.

• At the beginning of an interview, you and the respondent are strangers to each other.


• The respondent's first impression of you will influence his/her willingness to cooperate with the survey.

• Be sure that your manner is friendly as you introduce yourself. Make a good impression. When first approaching a respondent, do your best to make him/her feel safe and at ease. With a few-well-chosen words, you can put the respondent in the right frame of mind for the interview. Open the interview with a smile and greeting such as “good morning” and then proceed with your introduction. Always remember: - 10% of conflicts are due to difference in opinion. 90% are due to wrong tone of voice). always have a positive approach. Never adopt an apologetic manner, and do not use words such as “Are you too, busy?” Such questions invite refusal before you start. Rather, tell the respondent. “I would like to ask you a few questions” or “I would like to talk with you for a few minutes.”


• Maintain good eye contact. do not wander.

• Do not cross your arms. It comes off as defensive or uninterested.

• Practice your handshake, arrange your belongings properly.

• Avoid trying to cross your legs, this can cause distraction when switching legs.

• Keep an interested expression and do not forget to smile.

• Remember good posture and sit up straight.

• Do not overuse hand gestures, you do not want to distract from what you are saying.

• Remain calm, do not let nerves make your feet do a tap dance.

• Remember posture, gesture and expression make 55% of your message.

DATA COLLECTION

Data obtained must be complete and accurate, and as final as possible.


The data will be obtained from desk review of specific county budgets, national budget and from key informant interviews from ministry of health (MOH) through division of reproductive health(DRH), county treasuries or even county assemblies and county department of health through the office of health(COH),county health director and the county reproductive health coordinator.


KEY INFORMANT INTERVIEWS.

Key informant interviews will be carried on for key stakeholders on matters relating to FP budgets allocation at county and facilities level 4, level 3, level 2 will be

undertaken. This will involve 3 respondents per facility through KII or self- administered questionnaire.

Specifically, the KII will be conducted at facility level 4, 3, 2 in randomly selected facilities and purposefully selected respondents to establish the resultant benefit on public

FP budgeting especially on commodity availability, human resources, availability, human resources, availability of technical support through supportive supervision by the

CHMT and related activities.


The facility assessment will establish status in FP commodities availability and regularity of commodities supply.


DESKTOP REVIEW OF BACKGROUND LITERATURE AND STATISTICS.

This will entail vast documents of relevance to the review for instance.

  • • Policy paper, draft NHSSP 2
  • • Economic survey
  • • Medium term plan(MTP)
  • • Norms and standards for establishing HR requirements and commensurate HR budget allocation for FP services.
  • • Budget outlook paper (BOPA), medium term expenditure framework(MTEF), ministerial performance reviews(MPRs)
  • • Documents from CRA on approved county allocations.
  • • Other relevant documents as guided by the client.

STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT.

Focused group discussions (FDGs), we expect to have 44 FGDs per county with an assumption of 6-12 participants per FDG. The FDGs will target the community level

participants (1) Youth (2) Men (3) women (4) combined women and men participants.


The FGDs respondents will be selected through purposeful sampling to ensure they incorporate Healthworkers who deal with FP and RP services.


THE SCOPE AND DESIGN.

• The budget analysis will involve at least 11 counties i.e Kilifi, Mombasa, Nyandarua, Meru, Laikipia, Nakuru, Uasin gishu, Trans-nzoia, West pokot, Bungoma and Nandi.

• To achieve desired results, a sampling design together with a sample size, large enough to provide statistically significant estimates shall be designed.

• Transport reimbursement from home to meeting point and lunch of Kshs 1,000 per day.

• Transport within the county will be provided by the consulting team.

CHAPTER SEVEN

SYSTEM IMPLEMENTATION

INTRODUCTION

This the process within which an organization makes the system available to its users and positions ongoing support and maintenance of the system. There are many strategies that can be used Such as parallel changeover, phased, direct



With this strategy, the changeover is done in one operation, completely replacing the old system in one go. This usually takes place on a set date, often after a break in production or a holiday period so that time can be used to get the hardware and software for the new system installed without causing too much disruption.



  • • The most rapid of all the strategies, provided it works!
  • • Less risk of confusion between old and new systems.
  • • Most stressful for the users - the old system has gone so there is no going back if they have difficulties.
  • • Most difficult to train staff on as the new system was not in place to learn on before the changeover.
  • • Most stressful for the developers - all the data and files from the old system will have to be converted ready for use on the new one.
  • • Most risky - if the new system does not work properly then there is nothing to fall back on.

With this strategy, the new brought in stages. If each phase is successful then the next phase is started, eventually leading to the final phase when the new system fully replaces the old one.


  • • Very structured, each phase can be fully evaluated before moving onto the next one.
  • • Lower risk, a well-planned and controlled introduction of the new system.
  • • Easy to train staff by letting them learn new skills on each phase as it is introduced.

  • • Slower than direct implementation.
  • • Although each phase is easy to evaluate, you have to wait until all the phases are complete before you can evaluate the whole change over.

With this strategy, the new system replaces the old one in one operation but only on a small scale. For example, it might be tried out in one branch of the company or in one location. If successful, then the pilot is extended until it eventually replaces the old system completely.


  • • Easy to control, the pilot can be halted at any time.
  • • Easy to evaluate because the new and old systems are both running.
  • • Low risk, if a small-scale pilot fails then not too much has been lost.
  • • Easy to train staff by letting them learn new skills on the pilot system.
  • • It can be slow to get a pilot to completely replace the old system.
  • • A pilot may not show up problems that a full-scale implementation would. This is because a system can work well as a small-scale pilot but has difficulties when it is scaled up to a full operating system with more realistic volumes of data to be processed.

With this strategy, the old and the new system are both used alongside each other, both being able to operate independently. If all goes well, the old system is stopped and new system carries on as the only system.


  • • If there are initial problems with the new system, then the old one can still be used.
  • • Both systems can easily be compared.
  • • Easy to train staff by letting them learn new skills on the parallel system.
  • • Easy to evaluate because the new and old systems are both running.
  • • Expensive - both systems are being run as fully operating versions so both are doing the same job. This may mean duplication of staff and hardware.
  • • Some risk - there is a greater chance of confusion or errors if the two different systems are being run side-by-side.

Strategy used: I will use phased implementation because it provides a low risk well planned and controlled introduction of the new system and it it is easy to train staff by letting them learn new skills on each phase as it is introduced.
